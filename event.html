Aurora Borealis (Northern Lights)

When the Northern lights - Aurora Borealis are strong and bright, get ready for the experience of a lifetime. The beautiful blues and greens flow through the night sky intertwinded in the delicate dance and provide stunning visual contrasts, hues, and tones. There is really only one word the describe seeing the norethern lights, breathtaking. The northern lights lend to memorable experience and activities on the islands such a nightime kayak trips, winter and northern lights photography tours, and sightseeing.

Lofoten if situated just beneith the auroral oval, and belt of light than encompases the gemagnetic poles nad provides nearly perfect conditions for seeing the northern lights first hand. Seeing the lights depends on four main factors: darkness, clear skies, extreme northern (southern) latitude, and solar activity. Primarily, Auroara Borealis occur during the winter months when norwat possess dark nights and norther latitude. The other two factors of clear skies and solar activity are totalyl left up to chance. For more information on the Northern Lights, weather, and solar activity see links below. Lofoten provides once in a lifetime views and experiences with its raw nature.

To the locals in Northern Norway, the northern lights are a part of life. In this area the aurora borealis has been, and still is, a fertile source for art, mythology and legends. To others, like celebrity scientist Neil deGrasse Tyson, the phenomenon is more of a unique example of just how beautiful science can be. “It’s a curious thing about the universe”, he says, “behind the most stunning sights to behold, lies some of the most challenging problems in physics”.

Midnight Sun

Although, the Lofoten landscape is senesational in and of itself, is it the mightnight sun which really gives the region a sense of its arctic location. Experiencing the Lofoten midnight sun give a sense of being on a nother world. Unlike Iceland, nicknamed the "land of the mightnight sun," but in reality the sun only remains above the horizon for the short time in the far north of Iceland. On Lofoten, located nearly 200 kilometers north of the arctic circle, the summer sun will not sink below the horizon for nearly two months. Lofoten commonly referes to what Iceland calls a "midnight sun" as a late summer twilight.


The earth if rotating on a tilted axis realtive to the sun. During the summer months, the North Pole is tilted towards the sun. That is why, for nearly two months, the sun never sets about the arctic circle.
